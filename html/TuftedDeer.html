Tufted Deer

Meet the Tufted Deer, or Elaphodus cephalophus, a species indigenous to southern China and Myanmar (Burma). They get their name from a distinctive patch of poofy, dark fur on their heads. Hence “tufted.” This, however, is not the most striking feature of these little mammals- all the males grow sizable tusks from their upper jaw. Why the tuft is what they get their name from is BEYOND me. (It’s actually not. There are multiple species of tufted deer, this species just happens to have the tusks.)

Scientists believe these deer all share a common ancestor possessing both antlers and tusks. Indeed, the tufted deer does have very small antlers still, but they are hidden by their characteristic poof of fur. For the tufted deer, at least, the antlers were a hinderance in the dense vegetation of their habitat, liable to be tangled in the fauna. Tusks don’t have this problem and are equally useful. The males use their tusks similar to how other species use their antlers- for fighting and intimidation. This can mean other male deer (who they defend their territory against vigorously) or even predators. They are not, however, used for hunting. As exciting as a carnivorous deer would be, these deer are herbivores like all the others. Aside from their tusks, they have no upper teeth, and instead grind their food with their lower teeth against calloused pads on their upper jaws. Nor are they aggressive. Still very much deer, they are very shy creatures who prefer to travel in covered areas to hide and protect themselves from predators (mostly leopards and dholes, a type of wild dog native to Asia). Naturally then, they are more active at dawn, dusk, and the nighttime hours, preferring to hide during the day. For this reason, they are not often seen in the wild the way many bird species might be. While not endangered yet, the IUCN Red List has them down as “near threatened” as their population continues to shrink. Because of human encroachment, the size of their habitats is diminishing, and they are often hunted for their fur and meat. They tend to stick to valleys of high elevation and forested areas of mountains. Unless killed earlier by disease or predators, these deer can live up to 15 years. Shortly before they reach 2 years, they are ready to breed, at which point the females will produce 1-2 babies each year.
